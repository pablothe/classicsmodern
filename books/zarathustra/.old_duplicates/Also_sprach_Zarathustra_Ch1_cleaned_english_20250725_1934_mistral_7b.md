# Prologue: The Despisers of the Body

I

In solitude I sought myself; and now, lo! I find myself alone with my spirit—alone, and more alone than ever before.

II

The despisers of the body are all the more profoundly afraid that their own bodies will betray them: for _they_ are not whole, they are a riddle with many solutions—a secret that cannot be solved.

III

In solitude I sought myself; and now, lo! I find myself alone with my spirit—alone, and more alone than ever before.

IV

And now, when I see the despisers of the body, my contempt for them is as great as my pity for them: they are the ungrateful, for _they_ have not learned to love their own bodies, nor even to understand them—and yet they are bound to them with a more subtle bond than any other living thing upon earth.

V

Thus I say unto you, O my brothers: be lovers of yourselves, first and last! And those who have long despaired of finding themselves, as I did once, may perhaps learn from me.

VI

For even the spirit desires a body, and with a burning desire: this is the great hunger that no man can escape, not even the wisest and mightiest.

VII

The body is _not_ evil, and the soul _is_ not pure—but both are entangled in one another. The body _does_ sin, but the soul _also_ sins: for it was a long time ago when the soul first became flesh.

VIII

But, O my brothers, what need have I of your pity? For even today I am still the teacher of the despisers of the body—and tomorrow I shall be their avenger!

IX

And now my heart is weary and my spirit languishes: for in the desert of solitude much waterless ground lies before me. But _thus_ have I spoken unto you, and _thus_ shall I remain silent with you a while—until the hour strikes again when I must rise and speak to men.

# Chapter 5: On Friedrich Wilhelm Nietzsche

**5.1 Prelude**

In the annals of philosophy, few figures have cast such a long and lasting shadow as the German philosopher, Friedrich Wilhelm Nietzsche. Born in the year 1844 in Rocken, a small town in Saxony, his influence on Western thought remains unparalleled to this day.

**5.2 A Turbulent Childhood**

Nietzsche's early years were marked by tragedy and strife. Losing his mother at the tender age of four, he was raised by a distant, authoritarian father who instilled in him an iron will and a thirst for knowledge. However, this strict upbringing also left deep emotional scars that would resonate throughout his life and work.

**5.3 Education and Early Career**

After excelling at Pforta School, Nietzsche enrolled at the prestigious University of Bonn to study theology and classical philology. Intrigued by the philosophical debates of the time, he shifted his focus to philosophy under the tutelage of Friedrich Wilhelm Ritschl, a prominent figure in the field.

Upon completing his doctorate, Nietzsche took up a professorship at the University of Basel, where he remained for over a decade, producing some of his most influential works. Among them are 'The Birth of Tragedy' (1872) and 'Thus Spoke Zarathustra' (1883-1885).

**5.4 The Later Years**

In 1879, Nietzsche's health began to deteriorate, possibly due to a combination of factors including malnutrition and syphilis. This decline forced him to retire from teaching in 1879. His later years were characterized by increasing mental instability, culminating in a complete collapse in 1889. He spent the remainder of his life in various institutions until his death in 1900.

**5.5 Legacy and Influence**

Despite his personal struggles, Nietzsche's work continues to captivate readers and spark intense debate. His ideas on perspectivism, eternal recurrence, and the Übermensch (Superman) have left an indelible mark on literature, philosophy, and popular culture. Whether one agrees or disagrees with his ideas, there is no denying that Friedrich Wilhelm Nietzsche was a truly unique and groundbreaking thinker of his time.

### Birth and Early Life (1844 - 1864)

The philosopher Friedrich Wilhelm Nietzsche was born on October 15, 1844, in Röcken, a small town near Leipzig, Germany. His parents were Karl Ludwig Nietzsche, a Lutheran pastor, and Franziska Oehler.

Nietzsche attended a boarding school in Naumburg from the age of five, where he learned classical languages such as Greek and Latin. Later, he enrolled at Schulpforta, a famous Piarist school, to continue his education. Here, Nietzsche developed an interest in philosophy and poetry.

In 1864, Nietzsche's father died, leaving the family in financial trouble. Despite these hardships, Nietzsche managed to secure admission to the University of Bonn to study classical philology and theology.

### University Years (1865 - 1869)

During his time at the University of Bonn, Nietzsche became a member of the Burschenschaft der Dunne, a student fraternity. His affiliation with this group, however, was short-lived as he left after a dispute over politics.

In 1865, Nietzsche transferred to the University of Leipzig, where he focused on classical philology under the guidance of renowned scholars like Friedrich Albert Lange and Karl Wilhelm Friedrich Schmidt.

At Leipzig, Nietzsche formed close friendships with Richard Wagner's son-in-law, Cosima Wagner, and the composer Franz Liszt. These relationships would significantly influence his work in later years.

### Health Issues and Early Career (1869 - 1879)

In 1869, Nietzsche's health began to deteriorate due to a combination of physical exhaustion and psychological stress. He took a leave of absence from his studies at Leipzig and spent the following years recovering in Naumburg and Jena.

During this period, Nietzsche wrote essays on various topics, including Schopenhauer's philosophy, which were published in the "Luzifer" magazine. In 1870, he submitted his dissertation, "The Birth of Tragedy," for which he earned his doctorate.

Nietzsche accepted a position as a professor of classical philology at the University of Basel in Switzerland in 1869 and remained there until his retirement due to health issues in 1879. His work during this time included "Human, All Too Human," "Daybreak," and "The Gay Science."

### Later Life and Decline (1880 - 1900)

In the late 1870s, Nietzsche's health improved significantly, allowing him to travel extensively. He visited Italy, France, and Germany, meeting influential figures such as Paul Ree, Lou Salomé, and Heinrich Köselitz (also known as Peter Gast).

During this period, Nietzsche wrote some of his most famous works, including "Thus Spoke Zarathustra" and "Beyond Good and Evil." His work during the 1880s focused on the critical examination of morality, religion, and philosophical traditions.

In 1889, Nietzsche's mental health began to deteriorate, likely due to a combination of factors such as his intense work ethic, physical exhaustion, and syphilis. He spent the remainder of his life in various sanatoriums until his death on August 25, 1900.

### Legacy

Nietzsche's ideas continue to influence modern thought and philosophy. His concepts such as "perspectivism," "eternal recurrence," and the "will to power" have been widely debated and interpreted by scholars and philosophers worldwide. Nietzsche is often regarded as one of the most significant thinkers in Western philosophy.
