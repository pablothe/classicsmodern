#### 1.
When Zarathustra turned thirty, he left his home and the lake near it and went into the mountains. There, he enjoyed his thoughts and solitude for ten years without getting tired of it. However, eventually, his heart changed—and one morning, he woke up with the dawn, stood before the sun, and spoke to it like this:

“You great star! What would your happiness be if you didn’t have those to whom you shine? For ten years, you have risen here to my cave: you would have grown tired of your light and this path without me, my eagle, and my snake. But we waited for you every morning, took away your excess, and blessed you for it. Look! I am tired of my wisdom, like a bee that has collected too much honey; I need hands to reach out. I want to give and share until the wise among people once again find joy in their foolishness and the poor in their wealth. For this, I must go down into the depths: just like you do in the evening when you sink behind the sea and still bring light to the underworld, you very rich star! I must, like you, set, as people call it, to those I want to descend to. So bless me, you calm eye, that can see a happiness too great without envy!

Bless the cup that wants to overflow, so that golden water flows from it and spreads your joy everywhere! Look! This cup wants to be empty again, and Zarathustra wants to be human again. —This is how Zarathustra's downfall began.

#### 2. Zarathustra came down the mountain alone, and no one was there to meet him. But when he reached the woods, an old man suddenly appeared in front of him. He had left his sacred hut to look for roots in the forest. The old man then spoke to Zarathustra:
I recognize this wanderer: many years ago, he passed by here. His name was Zarathustra; but he has changed. Back then, you took your ashes to the mountains: do you want to bring your fire into the valleys today? Are you not afraid of being punished like an arsonist? Yes, I see Zarathustra. His eyes are clear, and there is no disgust in his mouth. Doesn’t he move like a dancer? Zarathustra has changed; he has become like a child, Zarathustra is awake: what do you want among those who are asleep now? You lived in solitude like the sea, and the sea supported you. Oh, do you want to come ashore? Oh, do you want to carry your body again on your own? Zarathustra replied: “I love humanity.”

Why, the holy man asked, did I go into the woods and the wilderness? Was it not because I loved humanity too much?

Now I love God, but I do not love humanity. Humanity is too flawed for me. Loving humanity would destroy me. Zarathustra responded, “What did I say about love? I bring humanity a gift.”

“Give them nothing,” said the holy man. “Take something from them and carry it with you—that will make them feel most at ease, as long as it benefits you! And if you want to give to them, then give only a little charity, and let them still have to ask for it!” “No,” replied Zarathustra, “I do not give charity. I am not poor enough for that.”

The saint laughed at Zarathustra and said, “Make sure they accept your gifts! They are wary of hermits and don’t believe we come to give. Our footsteps sound too lonely to them in the streets. Just like when they hear someone walking at night, long before dawn, they might wonder: where is the thief going? Don’t go to the people; stay in the forest! Go instead to the animals! Why don’t you want to be like me—a bear among bears, a bird among birds?”

“And what does the saint do in the forest?” asked Zarathustra. The saint answered, “I create songs and sing them, and when I create songs, I laugh, cry, and hum: this is how I praise God. Through singing, crying, laughing, and humming, I honor the God who is my God.”

But what do you bring us as a gift?" When Zarathustra heard this, he greeted the saint and said: "What could I give you! But let me hurry away, so I don’t take anything from you!"—And so they said goodbye to each other, the old man and the man, laughing like two boys. When Zarathustra was alone, he spoke to his heart: "Could it really be true! This old saint in his forest hasn’t heard yet that God is dead!"
