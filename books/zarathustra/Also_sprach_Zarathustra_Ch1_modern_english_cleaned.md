### Zarathustra's Foreword.
#### 1.
As Zarathustra turned thirty years old, he left his homeland and the lake of his homeland and went into the mountains. Here he enjoyed his spirit and his solitude and did not grow tired of it for ten years. Finally, however, his heart was transformed—and one morning he rose with the dawn, stepped before the sun, and spoke to it thus:

"Great star! What would your happiness be if you did not have those to whom you shine!

For ten years you came up here to my cave: you would have grown weary of your light and this path without me, my eagle, and my serpent.

But we awaited you every morning, took your abundance from you, and blessed you for it.

Behold! I am weary of my wisdom, like the bee that has gathered too much honey; I need hands that reach out.

I want to give and distribute until the wise among men once again rejoice in their folly and the poor in their riches.

For this, I must descend into the depths: as you do in the evening when you go behind the sea and still bring light to the underworld, you, exceedingly rich star!

I must, like you, go under, as men call it, to those I wish to descend to.

So bless me, you calm eye, that can see even a too-great happiness without envy!

Bless the cup that wants to overflow, so that the water flows golden from it and carries the reflection of your joy everywhere!"

See! This cup wants to be empty again, and Zarathustra wants to become human again.”

—Thus began Zarathustra’s downfall.

#### 2.
Zarathustra descended the mountain alone, and no one met him. But when he came to the woods, suddenly an old man stood before him, who had left his holy hut to seek roots in the forest. And thus spoke the old man to Zarathustra:

This wanderer is not strange to me: many years ago he passed by here. His name was Zarathustra; but he has transformed. Back then you carried your ashes to the mountains: do you want to carry your fire into the valleys today? Are you not afraid of the punisher of arsonists?

Yes, I recognize Zarathustra. Pure is his eye, and there is no disgust hidden in his mouth. Does he not move like a dancer?

Zarathustra has transformed, he has become a child, Zarathustra is an awakened one: what do you want among the sleeping?

As you lived in the sea in solitude, the sea carried you. Woe, do you want to climb ashore? Woe, do you want to carry your body again yourself?

Zarathustra answered: “I love humans.”

Why, said the saint, did I go into the woods and the wilderness? Was it not because I loved humans too much?

Now I love God: I do not love humans. Humanity is too imperfect a thing for me. Love for humans would kill me.

Zarathustra answered: “What did I say about love! I bring humanity a gift.”

Give them nothing, said the Saint. Rather take something from them and carry it with them—that will do them the most good: if it only benefits you!

And if you want to give to them, then give no more than a charity, and let them still beg for it!

“No,” replied Zarathustra, “I do not give charity. I am not poor enough for that.”

The Saint laughed at Zarathustra and said: So make sure they accept your treasures! They are suspicious of hermits and do not believe that we come to give.

Our steps sound too lonely to them through the streets. And just as when they hear a man walking at night in their beds, long before the sun rises, they may ask themselves: where does the thief want to go?

Do not go to the people and stay in the forest! Rather go to the animals! Why do you not want to be like me—a bear among bears, a bird among birds?

“And what does the Saint do in the forest?” asked Zarathustra.

The Saint replied: I make songs and sing them, and when I make songs, I laugh, cry, and hum: thus I praise God.

With singing, crying, laughing, and humming, I praise the God who is my God. But what do you bring us as a gift?

When Zarathustra had heard these words, he greeted the Saint and said: “What would I have to give you! But let me quickly go, so that I take nothing from you!”—And so they parted from each other, the old man and the man, laughing, just like two boys laugh.

As Zarathustra was alone, he spoke thus to his heart: "Could it really be possible! This old saint has not yet heard in his forest that God is dead!"—
