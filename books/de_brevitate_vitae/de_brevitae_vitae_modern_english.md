Lucius Annaeus Seneca  
On the Shortness of Life  
To Paulinus  
c. 49 AD  

Edition: L. Annaeus Seneca, On the Shortness of Life, in Moral Essays: volume 2, London and New York, Heinemann, 1932; Edited by John W. Basore  
Source: Corpus Corporum  
Index  
I. review  
[1] Most people, Paulinus, complain about the wickedness of nature, that we are born into such a brief existence, that the time given to us passes so quickly and swiftly, that except for very few, life leaves the rest of us wanting in the very arrangement of life itself. Nor is it only the common, thoughtless crowd that groans over this public misfortune; even distinguished men have expressed their complaints about it. Hence the famous doctors' exclamation: 'Life is short, but art is long'; [2] hence the dispute of Aristotle, which seems entirely incongruous for a wise man examining nature: 'It has granted animals a lifespan of five or ten centuries, but for man, born into so many great things, it has set a much shorter limit.' [3] We do not lack time, but we waste it. A sufficiently long life is generously given for the accomplishment of great things if it is well used; but when it flows away through luxury and negligence, when no good deed is undertaken, we eventually feel that time has passed without us even realizing it, driven by the final necessity. [4] Indeed, we do not receive a short life, but we make it so, and we are not lacking in it but are wasteful.

Just as great and royal wealth, when it falls into the hands of a bad master, is dissipated in an instant, so too, even modest resources, if entrusted to a good guardian, grow through use. Thus, our age offers much to those who manage it well.

What do we complain about regarding the nature of things? It has treated us kindly; life, if you know how to use it, is long. But one person is held by insatiable greed, another by busy and futile labors; one is drunk on wine, another is sluggish with idleness; one is worn out by the constant ambition for others' judgments, another is driven by a reckless desire for trade across all lands and seas, led by the hope of profit; some are tormented by the desire for military glory, always focused on the dangers of others or anxious about their own; there are those whom the ungrateful service of superiors consumes through voluntary servitude; many are held back by either the affectation of others' fortunes or complaints about their own; most are tossed about by an uncertain and inconsistent lightness, dissatisfied with themselves and thrown into new schemes; for some, nothing pleases them to guide their course, but they are overwhelmed by their fading and languid fates, to the point that I do not doubt the truth of what was said by the greatest of poets in the manner of an oracle: "A small part of life is what we live." Furthermore, indeed, all space is not life but time.

Vices press in from all sides, preventing us from rising or lifting our eyes to the truth, but they hold us down, plunged into desire. They can never return to themselves; whenever some chance of rest occurs, like a deep sea, even after a storm, they are tossed about and never find peace from their desires. Do you think I am speaking of those whose evils are obvious? Look at those whose happiness is sought after; they are suffocated by their own wealth. How many are burdened by riches!

How many people's eloquence and daily desire to show off their talent drains the blood! How many grow pale from constant pleasures! How many are left with nothing by the surrounding crowd of clients! Ultimately, all those from the lowest to the highest wander around: here someone advocates, there someone is present, one is in danger, another defends, another judges, no one claims themselves, another is consumed by another. If you ask about those whose names are memorized, you will see them recognized by these marks: that one is a follower of that one, this one is of another; no one belongs to themselves. Then there is the most foolish indignation of some: they complain about the disdain of their superiors, who do not have time for them wanting to approach! Does anyone dare to complain about another's arrogance, who never makes time for themselves? Yet, that one has looked at you, whoever you are, with a haughty face but has at times turned back, that one has listened to your words, that one has welcomed you to their side; you have never deigned to look at yourself or to listen. Thus, there is no reason to blame anyone for these duties, since indeed, when you did those things, you did not wish to be with another, but could not be with yourself.

III. To summarize
Although all the brilliant minds that ever existed may agree on this one thing, they will never cease to marvel at this darkness of human minds. They do not allow their property to be taken by anyone, and if there is a slight dispute about the boundaries, they rush to stones and weapons; they allow others to encroach on their lives, and in fact, they even bring in those who will become owners of it. No one can be found who wants to share their money; everyone distributes their life to as many as possible! They are bound in preserving their inheritance, while at the same time, they have come to waste time, being the most extravagant in that, whose honest greed is singular.

It is therefore pleasing to select someone from among the elders: "We see that you have reached the very end of human life; you are burdened by your hundredth year or more. Now, let’s calculate your age. Consider how much time you owe—how much to friends, how much to the king, how much to clients, how much to legal disputes with your wife, how much to the control of servants, how much to your busy wandering through the city. Add the illnesses we have caused ourselves and also what lay unused; you will find that you have fewer years than you count. 

Recall with yourself when you were certain in your decisions, how many days have passed as you intended, when you were truly engaged, when your expression was carefree, when your spirit was fearless, what you have accomplished in such a long life, how many have taken your life from you without you realizing what you were losing, how much empty pain, foolish joy, greedy desire, and charming conversation have taken away, and how little remains for you; you will understand that you might die too soon." 

What then is the reason? You live as if you will always survive, never considering your own fragility, not realizing how much time has already passed; as if from a full and overflowing cup, you are losing it, perhaps while that very day that is gifted to someone—be it a person or a thing—is the last. You desire everything. You will hear many say: "I will retire from my duties at fifty, the sixtieth year will free me from my responsibilities." And what, in the end, do you gain from a longer life? Who will allow you to go as you plan? Are you not ashamed to reserve the remnants of life for yourself, dedicating only that time which cannot be applied to anything worthwhile? How late it is to begin living when it is time to stop!

What a foolish forgetfulness of mortality to delay sound plans until the fifty-fifth or sixtieth year and then wish to begin life, which few have achieved! IV. To recount  
[1] You will see the voices of the most powerful men rise up, who desire leisure, praise it, and place it above all their goods. Meanwhile, they wish to descend from their lofty heights, if it is safe to do so; for fortune collapses upon itself, untouched by anything external. [2] The divine Augustus, who received more from the gods than anyone else, did not cease to pray for peace and seek a break from public affairs; his every conversation always revolved around hoping for leisure. This thought sweetly comforted him, even if falsely, that he might one day live for himself. [3] In a letter sent to the Senate, having promised that his rest would not be devoid of dignity nor different from his former glory, he wrote these words: "But such things seem more beautiful to be done than to be promised. Yet my longing for the most desired time has led me to think that since the joy of affairs still lingers, I should take some pleasure from the sweetness of words." [4] Leisure appeared so significant that, since it could not be experienced, he presumed it in thought. He saw everything depending on him alone, who granted fortune to peoples and nations, and he thought of the happiest day when he would shed his greatness. He had experienced how much those glowing goods in all lands demanded sweat and how much they concealed hidden anxieties. [5] With citizens first, then with his colleagues, and finally forced by relatives, he fought with arms at sea and on land, shedding blood. Through Macedonia, Sicily, Egypt, Syria, Asia, and nearly all coasts, he turned his weary armies, worn from Roman bloodshed, to foreign wars.

While the Alps are peaceful and enemies are being subdued in the midst of this peace and authority, while he even moves the boundaries beyond the Rhine, Euphrates, and Danube, in the very city, the weapons of Murena, Caepio, Lepidus, Egnatius, and others were being sharpened against him. He had not yet escaped their traps: his daughter and many noble young men, driven by infidelity as if by a vow, were already terrifying him in his old age, and again, the woman whom he feared was with Antony. These wounds had cut him at his very limbs: others were springing up; it was as if a heavy body was always breaking apart with much blood from some part. Therefore, he wished for peace, and in this hope and thought, his labors were resting; this was the vow of one who could fulfill such a vow.

M. Cicero, tossed among Catilinas, Clodios, and the likes of Pompey and Crassus—some openly hostile, some uncertain allies—while he struggles with the state and holds onto it as it is going badly, was recently led away, neither at peace with favorable circumstances nor able to bear adverse ones, whenever he detests his own consulship, praised not without cause but endlessly! How sorrowful his words are in a certain letter to Atticus, after Pompey had been defeated, with the son still renewing arms in Spain! "What should I do," he says, "here, you ask? I linger in my semi-free Tusculum." He then adds other things, lamenting his earlier years, complaining about the present, and despairing of the future. Cicero called himself semi-free. But I swear by Hercules, a wise man will never take such a low title, he will never be semi-free, always possessing complete and solid freedom, unbound and self-governing, and higher than others. For what can be above him who is above fortune?

### Translation

**Review**

Livius Drusus, a strong and passionate man, stirred up new laws and the troubles of the Gracchi while surrounded by a huge crowd from all over Italy. Not foreseeing the outcome of events, which he could neither act upon nor leave unfinished, he is said to have cursed his restless life from the beginning, declaring that he had never had a holiday, not even as a child. He even dared to commend defendants to the judges while still a minor and to insert his influence into the forum so effectively that certain judgments are reported to have been taken from him. 

How could such premature ambition not burst forth? You would know that this reckless audacity would lead to great private and public harm. Therefore, he later lamented that he had never had a holiday since childhood, being contentious and heavy in the forum. It is debated whether he took his own life; for he collapsed suddenly from a wound to his groin, with some questioning whether his death was voluntary, and no one knowing if it was timely.

It is unnecessary to mention the many who seemed happier than him; they themselves declared true testimony against all their years, having grown weary of every action of their lives, but these complaints changed neither others nor themselves. For his words burst forth, and his feelings slipped back into habit.

By Hercules, your life, even if it extends over a thousand years, will be drawn into the tightest confines; these vices will consume every age. Indeed, this time, which nature may run through, reason expands, must quickly escape you; for you neither grasp nor hold on, nor do you delay the swiftest of all things, but you allow it to depart as a superfluous and reparable matter.

VII. **Review**

Firstly, let us consider those who occupy themselves with nothing but wine and lust; for none are more shamefully engaged.

The others, even if they are held by the empty image of glory, still err in a grand way; whether you count the greedy, the angry, or those who exercise unjust hatred or war, all of these are flawed in their strengths; the shameful indulgence of their bellies is a disgrace. [2] Look at all their times, see how long they calculate, how long they plot, how long they fear, how long they worship, how long they are worshipped, how much they occupy their own and other people's debts, how much of their banquets, which are already duties themselves: you will see how neither their own bad nor good allow them to breathe. [3] Ultimately, it is agreed among all that no matter can be well managed by a busy person; neither eloquence nor the liberal arts can be properly learned, since a distracted mind cannot take in anything deeper, but instead rejects everything as if it were pressed upon them. Nothing is less characteristic of a busy person than living; no knowledge is harder to acquire. Professors of other arts are numerous, and many have seemed to learn some of these arts so well that they could even teach them. Living is something to be learned throughout life, and, perhaps more surprisingly, learning to die is also something to be learned throughout life. [4] Many great men, having set aside all distractions, renounced wealth, duties, and pleasures, did this one thing until the very end of their lives: they learned how to live; however, many of them confessed they did not know how to live before they passed away, let alone those who do not know. [5] Believe me, a great man, and one who rises above human errors, does not allow anything from his time to be wasted, and that is why his life is the longest; as much as he could, he devoted his entire time to himself. Nothing remained uncultivated or idle, nothing was placed under another's care, for he found nothing worthy enough to exchange with his precious time.

Therefore, it is enough for him; however, those who have greatly contributed to the life of the people must necessarily lack. [6] And there is no reason to think that they do not sometimes realize their loss. You will certainly hear many of those burdened by great fortune crying out among their clients or in the midst of legal actions or other respectable miseries: "I cannot live." [7] Why shouldn't they be able to live? All those who call you to them are pulling you away. How many days has that defendant taken away? How many has that candidate? How many has that old woman, worn out from handing down her inheritance? How many has that sick person, acting to provoke the greed of those who are waiting? How many has that more powerful friend, who sees you not as a friend but as a resource? I say, analyze and review the days of your life; you will see that very few and insignificant moments have actually remained with you. [8] That one, having achieved the offices he desired, wishes to place them down and continually says: "When will this year pass?" He puts on games, which he greatly values to win: "When," he says, "will I escape from these?" That patron is being torn apart in the whole forum and fills everything beyond what can be heard: "When," he says, "will things be brought forth?" Everyone rushes their life and struggles with the desire for the future, while being bored with the present. [9] But the one who makes use of every moment, who organizes each day as if it were the last, neither hopes for tomorrow nor fears it. For what could any hour bring that would offer new pleasures? Everything is familiar, everything has been experienced to the point of saturation. From now on, let fortune arrange things as it wishes; life is already secure.

To this, nothing can be taken away, and it can be added in the same way as someone who is already full and satisfied can still take in more food, which they neither desire nor need. Therefore, you should not think that anyone has lived long simply because they have gray hair or wrinkles; they did not live long, they merely existed for a long time. For what if you think that someone has sailed a lot when a fierce storm has tossed them back and forth from the port, carried about by the raging winds through the same spaces all over the world? They did not sail much; they were just thrown around a lot.

1. I often wonder when I see some people seeking time and those who are asked are so accommodating. Both are looking at the reason why time is sought, yet neither seems to consider it; as if nothing is being sought, as if nothing is given. The most precious thing of all is being played with; however, they are deceived because it is incorporeal, because it does not come before their eyes, and therefore it is valued as almost worthless, or even has no value at all. 
2. People receive annual bonuses and offer their labor, effort, or diligence in exchange. No one values time; they use it freely as if it costs nothing. But see the same sick people when the danger of death is close, touching the knees of doctors if they fear a capital punishment; they are ready to spend everything they have to live! 
3. Such a great discord of feelings exists in them. If the number of past years could be shown, so too could the future; then those who saw only a few years left would tremble, and they would be more cautious! Yet it is easy to manage even a small amount of what is certain; that which you do not know when it will run out should be preserved more carefully. 
4. However, you should not think that they are unaware of how precious this thing is; they often tell those they love the most that they are ready to give a part of their years.

They give but do not understand; they give in such a way that they take away from themselves without their own increase. But they do not even know if they are taking away; therefore, they tolerate the loss of hidden detriment. No one can restore years, no one can give you back to yourself again. Time will go as it began, and it will not recall or slow its course; nothing will be disturbed, nothing will remind it of its speed. It will slip away quietly; it will not be extended by the king's command, nor by the people's favor. Just as it was sent forth from the first day, it will run; it will not turn aside anywhere, nor will it linger. What will happen? You are busy, life is hurrying; meanwhile, death will be present, to whom, whether you want it or not, you must give your attention. 

1. Can anything be more foolish than the views of certain men, those who boast of their wisdom? They are busy with cumbersome tasks to live better; they spend their lives arranging life itself! They organize their thoughts for the long term; the greatest waste of life is delay; it first drags each person through the day, it snatches away the present while promising future benefits. The greatest obstacle to living is expectation, which hangs on tomorrow, losing today. What is in the hands of fortune, you plan; what is in your hands, you let go. What are you looking at? Where are you reaching? All that is to come lies uncertain; live straight away! 

2. Behold, the greatest prophet cries out as if inspired by divine breath, singing a salutary song: "The best days flee first from miserable mortals." "Why do you hesitate?" he says, "Why do you delay? Unless you seize it, it escapes." And even if you seize it, it will still flee; thus, with the speed of time, you must compete with its swiftness, as if drawing quickly from a rushing stream that will not always flow. 

3. This also beautifully highlights the endless procrastination, as it speaks not of the best age but of the day.

What is the point of stretching out your months and years in such a long line, as you see fit for your desires, while time flies by? The day speaks with you about this very thing that is escaping. Is there any doubt that the best days flee from miserable mortals, that is, from those who are preoccupied? Their youthful spirits are crushed by old age, to which they arrive unprepared and defenseless, for nothing has been planned; they suddenly fall into it without warning, and they do not feel its approach each day. Just as a conversation, reading, or some focused thought distracts a traveler, making them feel they have arrived before they actually have, so this journey of life, both constant and swift, which we undertake whether awake or asleep, only becomes apparent to the preoccupied at the end.

If I want to break down what I proposed into parts and develop the arguments, many points will arise to show that the lives of the preoccupied are very brief. Fabianus, not one of those academic philosophers, but a true and ancient thinker, used to say: "Against the assault of emotions, one must fight with force, not subtlety; one should not inflict minor wounds but rather divert the attack with a strong blow; I do not approve of quibbling, for one must be crushed, not just pinched." Yet, in order for their error to be pointed out, they must be taught, not just lamented.

Life is divided into three times: what has been, what is, and what will be. Of these, what we are doing is brief, what we will do is uncertain, and what we have done is certain. This is what fate has lost control over, and it cannot be brought under anyone's judgment. The preoccupied lose this; they do not have the time to look back at the past, and even if they do, the memory of regrettable things is unpleasant.

3 Therefore, those unwilling recall their minds to times poorly spent and do not dare to revisit those things, the faults of which, even if they were stolen away by some present pleasure, become evident through reflection. No one, except for the person to whom everything is subject to his own judgment, which never fails, willingly looks back into the past; 4 that person who ambitiously desired many things, arrogantly scorned them, uncontrollably conquered, deceitfully tricked, greedily seized, and lavishly squandered, must fear for his own memory. Yet this is the sacred and dedicated part of our time, having surpassed all human misfortunes, withdrawn from the realm of fortune, which is not disturbed by poverty, fear, or the onslaught of diseases; this cannot be disturbed or taken away: it is a perpetual and fearless possession. Each day is present only for a moment; but all of the past will be there when you command, they will allow themselves to be seen and held at your discretion, which you cannot do if you are busy. 5 It is the mark of a secure and tranquil mind to wander through all parts of one's life; the minds of those who are occupied, as if under a yoke, cannot bend or look back. Therefore, their lives sink into the depths; and just as nothing is beneficial, no matter how much you may accumulate, if there is nothing to receive and preserve it, so it matters not how much time is given if there is nowhere for it to settle, it is passed through shaken and pierced souls. 6 The present time is extremely brief, so much so that for some it seems nonexistent; for it is always in motion, flowing and rushing; it ceases to be before it arrives, nor does it allow any delay more than the world or the stars, whose restless agitation never remains in the same place.

The present time only concerns those who are busy, which is so brief that it cannot be grasped, and even this is often taken away from them in many ways. 

XI. Do you want to know how little time they live? Look at how much they wish to live long. Frail old men beg for just a few more years; they pretend to be younger than they are; they flatter themselves with lies and deceive themselves as willingly as if fate itself were tricking them. When some weakness of mortality reminds them of their condition, they die not so much as if leaving life, but as if being pulled away from it. They shout that they were foolish for not having lived, and if they manage to escape that illness, they think they will live in peace; then they realize how futile their preparations were, how much effort fell to nothing when they cannot enjoy it. But for those whose lives are free from all business, why shouldn't they have spacious time? Nothing is taken from that life, nothing is scattered here and there, nothing is handed over to chance, nothing is lost to negligence, nothing is taken away by generosity, nothing is superfluous; all of it, so to speak, is in the return. Therefore, whatever little is sufficient, when the last day comes, the wise person will not hesitate to go to death with certainty.

XII. Do you perhaps ask who I call busy? Do not think I mean only those whom the dogs driven from the church finally chase away, those you see either more splendidly crushed in their own crowd or more contemptibly in someone else's, those whom the duties of their homes call away to batter at foreign doors, or those who exercise infamy and profit under the spear of the praetor, sometimes even profiting from it.

Some people are busy with leisure; in the villa or in their bed, in the midst of solitude, even though they have withdrawn from everyone, they are a burden to themselves. Their life is not to be called idle, but rather a lazy occupation. Do you call him idle who, in Corinth, entangled in the madness of a few, crafts with anxious precision and spends most of his days on greenish sheets? Who sits as a spectator of boys fighting, in wax (for heaven's sake! not even the Romans are free from faults)? Who sorts his livestock by age and color? Who feeds the newest athletes? What about those you call idle, who spend many hours at the barber, while their hair is trimmed, if anything has grown back since the previous night, while deliberating over each individual hair, either restoring a disheveled hairstyle or forcing back the strands that are falling out? How angry they get if the barber is a bit careless, as if he were shearing a man! How they fume if something is cut from their mane, if anything lies out of place, unless everything falls back into their curls! Who among them would rather see the public disturbed than their own hair? Who is more concerned about the style of their hair than about their safety? Who would prefer to be more styled than more honorable? Do you call them idle while they are occupied between comb and mirror? What about those who are engaged in composing, listening to, and learning songs, while they twist a voice that nature made straight, best, and simplest into the most languid modulation, whose fingers always sound out some measure of a song, and when they are often called upon for serious matters, even sad ones, their silent modulation is heard? These do not have leisure, but a lazy business.

5 By Hercules, I would not place these banquets among idle times, when I see how anxiously they arrange their silver, how carefully they tuck in the tunics of their male prostitutes, how tense they are, as a wild boar leaves the meal, how swiftly, at the given signal, the clean-shaven rush to their duties, how skillfully they chop the birds into small pieces, and how diligently the unfortunate little boys wipe away the spit of the drunkards. From these, the fame of elegance and luxury is captured, and to such an extent do their troubles follow them into every retreat of life that they neither drink without ambition nor eat.

6 I would not even count among the idle those who carry themselves in chairs and litters here and there and, as if they cannot abandon them, they mark the hours for their journeys. Someone reminds them when they should wash, when to swim, when to dine; they have become so excessively delicate and weak in spirit that they cannot even tell if they are hungry!

7 I hear of a certain delicate man—if indeed we can call living a life of indulgence and losing touch with humanity—who, after being lifted from the bath and seated in a chair, asked while questioning: “Am I already sitting?” Do you think he, unaware of whether he is sitting, knows if he is alive, if he sees, or if he is idle? I could not easily say whether I should feel more pity for him if he truly did not know or if he pretended not to know.

8 Indeed, they feel the forgetfulness of many things, but they also imitate many. Some vices delight them as if they were signs of happiness; it seems too lowly and contemptible for a man to know what he is doing. Go on, and imagine that the mimes tell many lies to highlight their luxury. By Hercules, more things pass me by than they pretend, and such an incredible abundance of vices has emerged in this one age that we can now accuse the mimes of negligence.

---

Is there someone who has indulged so much in pleasures that he believes he can sit idle while another does? Therefore, he is not idle; give him another name; he is sick, in fact, he is dead; the one who is truly idle is the one who has time for leisure and awareness. However, this person is half-alive, who needs a guide to understand the conditions of his own body; how can he be a master of any time?

1. It is lengthy to follow each individual, whose lives have been consumed by either games or the care of their bodies under the sun. They are not idle, whose pleasures involve much activity. For no one will doubt that those who are engaged in the study of useless literature do nothing productive, which is now also a significant pursuit among the Romans. 
2. This sickness of the Greeks was to inquire how many rowers Ulysses had, whether the Iliad or the Odyssey was written first, and whether they were by the same author, and other questions of this kind, which whether you keep to yourself or express, do not help your silent conscience; they do not make you wiser but rather more bothersome. 
3. Behold, the empty pursuit of learning the unnecessary has also invaded the Romans. These days, I heard someone recounting what each of the first Roman leaders had done; the first to win a naval battle was Duilius, the first to lead elephants in triumph was Curius Dentatus. Even now, these matters, although they do not lead to true glory, revolve around examples of civic achievements; such knowledge is not beneficial, yet it holds us captive with the pleasing vanity of things. 
4. Let us also dismiss the inquiry of who first persuaded the Romans to board a ship.

---

Claudius was called "Caudex" for this reason: the context of multiple tablets is called "caudex" in ancient times, from which public records are referred to as "codices," and ships that transport goods along the Tiber are still called "codicariae" due to this ancient custom. It is also relevant that Valerius Corvinus was the first to conquer Messana and, being the first from the Valerio family to have the name Messana after the city was captured, he gradually became known to the public as "Messala" as the letters changed. 

Do you allow anyone to care about the fact that L. Sulla was the first to release lions in the circus, when otherwise they were kept tied up, to finish them off using throwers sent from King Bocchus? And this should indeed be set aside: is it relevant that Pompey was the first to stage a fight with eighteen elephants in the circus, with men charged with harmful battle tactics? The leader of the state and among the ancient leaders, as tradition holds, thought it remarkable to lose men in such a spectacle in a new way. Do they fight? That is not enough. Do they rage? That is not enough: they are destroyed by the enormous mass of animals! It would have been better for this to be forgotten so that no powerful person would later learn and envy such a completely inhumane act. Oh, how much darkness does great fortune cast upon our minds! He believed he was above the nature of things when he threw so many miserable crowds of men to be slaughtered by beasts born under another sky, when he engaged in war between such different animals, pouring out much blood before the sight of the Roman people, soon to be forced to pour out even more blood himself. But later, deceived by the treachery of Alexandria, he offered himself to be stabbed in the last tribute, finally realizing the emptiness of his boastful name.

8 But to return to where I left off, I will show the unnecessary diligence of some regarding the same subject: it was said that Metellus, triumphant over the defeated Carthaginians in Sicily, led one hundred and twenty captured elephants in front of his chariot, the only Roman to do so; Sulla, the last of the Romans, extended the pomerium, which was never customary to expand into provincial lands, but rather into lands acquired within Italy, according to ancient tradition. It is more beneficial to know this than to assert that the Aventine Hill lies outside the pomerium, as he claimed, for one of two reasons: either because the common people had withdrawn there, or because the birds did not signal in that place under the auspices of Remus, along with countless other reasons that are either filled with lies or similar? 9 For even if we concede that they speak in good faith, as they write for performance, whose errors will diminish? Whose desires will they suppress? Who will they make stronger, more just, or more generous? In the meantime, our Fabian was uncertain whether it would be better to engage in no studies at all than to become entangled in these.

1 The only ones who are truly idle are those who are devoted to wisdom; they alone live, for they do not merely protect their own time well. They add to every age; whatever years have passed before them, they have gained for themselves. Unless we are the most ungrateful, these illustrious founders of sacred beliefs were born for us, preparing life for us. We are led from the darkest places to the brightest things through the labor of others; no age is forbidden to us, we are admitted into all things, and if we wish to transcend the limits of human weakness with great ambition, there is much time to explore. 2 It is permissible to argue with Socrates, to doubt whether he rests with Carneades, to conquer human nature with the Stoics, or to surpass it with the Cynics.

As the nature of things allows us to proceed through all time, why shouldn’t we devote ourselves entirely to what is vast and eternal, which is shared with the better aspects of existence? Those who are busy with various duties, who disturb themselves and others, even after they have acted madly and have walked through the thresholds of all, neglecting no open doors, after they have delivered their obligatory greetings through the most diverse homes, how many of them will be able to see anyone amidst such a vast city filled with various desires? How many will be kept away by sleep, luxury, or inhumanity! How many, after long tormenting them, will rush past with feigned urgency! How many will avoid emerging through a crowded atrium filled with clients and will flee through dark entrances of houses, as if it is less inhumane to deceive than to exclude! How many, half-asleep and heavy from yesterday’s drinking, will wait for those poor souls, breaking their own sleep, and with barely lifted lips will mumble the name of their host in the most arrogant yawn! 

We may say that these are the true duties of those who wish to be close to Zeno, Pythagoras, Democritus, and the other masters of the good arts, including Aristotle and Theophrastus. None of them will be idle, none will let anyone leave who is happier and more affectionate towards them, nor will anyone allow anyone to depart empty-handed. They can meet at night and be free from all mortals during the day.

No one will force you to die among these; all will teach you. None of them will waste your years but will contribute their own to you; no conversation will be dangerous, no friendship lethal, no costly observation. You will take from them whatever you want; through them, nothing will prevent you from taking as much as you desire.

2 What happiness awaits him, what a beautiful old age remains for the one who has committed himself to their care! He will have those with whom he can deliberate on both the smallest and the greatest matters, whom he can consult daily, from whom he can hear the truth without insult, be praised without flattery, and to whose likeness he can aspire. 3 We often say that it was not in our power to choose our parents, as they were given to us by chance; however, we can choose how to be born. They come from families of the noblest talents; you can choose into which one you wish to be adopted; you will not only be adopted into a name but into the very goods, which will not be kept poorly or grudgingly; they will become greater the more you share them with others. 4 They will give you a path to eternity and will lift you to that place from which no one falls. This is the one way to extend mortality, or rather to transform it into immortality. Honors, monuments, anything that ambition has commanded through decrees or constructed through works, quickly falls apart; nothing escapes the erosion of time, which moves everything. But what wisdom has consecrated cannot be harmed; no age will erase it, nor will it diminish it; the next and always subsequent time will add something to its reverence, since envy operates in close proximity, while we marvel at what is placed far away. 5 Therefore, the life of a wise person is greatly expanded; it does not end for him in the same way it does for others. He alone is released from the laws of humanity; all ages serve him like a god. Does time pass? He captures it with memory. Is it pressing? He makes use of it. Is it coming? He anticipates it. The gathering of all times creates for him a long life in one. 1 Their lives are the shortest and most anxious, those who forget the past, neglect the present, and fear the future; when they reach the end, they understand too late, poor souls, that they have been occupied for so long while doing nothing.

2 Nor should you think that this argument proves they lead a long life just because they sometimes call for death. They are troubled by uncertainty and unexpected feelings, fearing what they encounter; they often wish for death because they are afraid. 3 This argument also does not support the idea that they live long lives, as they often perceive long days, complaining that time moves slowly when the appointed hour for dinner arrives; for whenever they are free from their tasks, left in idleness, they become restless and do not know how to organize or make the most of their time. Therefore, they pursue some activity, and any time in between feels burdensome; by Hercules, it is as if they want to skip over the days when a gladiatorial game is announced, or when the time for another show or pleasure is awaited. Any delay in what they hope for feels long to them. 4 But the time they love is short, and it becomes even shorter due to their own faults; for they flee from one desire to another and cannot stay focused on one. Days do not feel long to them, but rather unbearable; in contrast, how short the nights seem, which they spend in the embrace of courtesans or in wine! 5 Hence, the madness of poets feeds on human errors in their stories, where Jupiter, seduced by the pleasure of coupling, seems to have doubled the night. What else is it but to ignite our vices than to inscribe gods as their authors and give the disease an excuse through divine permissiveness? Those nights, which they deserve so dearly, might not seem very short to them. They lose the day in anticipation of the night, and the night in fear of the light. XVII. review.  
1 Their pleasures are anxious and restless due to various fears, and suddenly, amidst their greatest joy, a worried thought arises: "How long will this last?"

"From this feeling, kings wept for their power, nor were they pleased by the greatness of their fortune, but the future end frightened them. When the most arrogant king of the Persians spread his army over the vast fields, he did not grasp its number but its measure, and he shed tears, knowing that within a hundred years, no one from such a youth would survive. 2 But it was fate itself that he was about to bring upon those he wept for, some to perish at sea, others on land, some in battle, others in flight, and within a short time, he was to consume those whom he feared would reach their hundredth year. 3 What of the fact that their joys are also trembling? For they do not rest on solid grounds but are disturbed by the same vanity from which they arise. What do you think these times are like, even by their own admission miserable, when these things, by which they elevate themselves and boast over others, are hardly sincere? 4 The greatest goods are anxious, nor is any fortune believed to be less well off than the best; another happiness is needed to maintain happiness, and for the very wishes that have succeeded, new wishes must be made. For anything that comes by chance is unstable; the higher it rises, the more likely it is to fall. No one delights in the idea of falling; therefore, it is necessary for the lives of those who prepare with great labor for what they possess to be not only the shortest but also the most miserable. They work hard to obtain what they want, anxiously holding onto what they have achieved; 5 meanwhile, there is never a chance to return to past times. New pursuits replace old ones, hope ignites hope, ambition fuels ambition. The end of sufferings is not sought, but the material changes. Have our honors tormented us? They take away more of our time. Do candidates stop working? We begin to court voters. Do we lay aside the trouble of accusations? We find ourselves in the burden of judgments. Does the judge cease to be? There is a seeker instead. Has the management of others' goods grown old through mercenary care? It is distinguished by its own resources."

6 Did Marius release his boot? He holds the consulship. Is Quintius eager to rush into the dictatorship? He will be called back from the plow. Scipio will go against the Carthaginians, though he is not yet mature enough for such a thing; the victor of Hannibal, the victor of Antioch, the glory of his consulship, a sponsor of his brother, unless there is a delay, he would be restored by Jupiter; civil unrest will agitate the savior, and after being worn out by the young man's disdain for equal honors, the ambition of an old man in exile will still delight him. There will never be a lack of causes for either happiness or miserable worries; life will be pushed along through occupations. Leisure will never be enjoyed; it will always be desired. 

18. Therefore, take yourself away from the crowd, dearest Paul, and finally retreat to a calmer harbor, no longer tossed about by the storms of life. Consider how many waves you have endured, how many tempests you have faced, both personal and public; enough virtue has already been shown through laborious and restless trials: experience what you can do in leisure. The greater part of your life, certainly better for the republic, has been given; take some of your time for yourself as well. 

2 I do not call you to a lazy or inert rest, nor do I want you to drown your lively spirit in sleep and the pleasures of the crowd. That is not true repose; you will find greater things than all the tasks you have handled so far, which you will pursue when you are relaxed and secure. 

3 Indeed, you manage the affairs of the world as carefully as if they were not your own, as diligently as your own, as devoutly as public matters. In your duty, you gain affection, where it is difficult to avoid hatred; but believe me, it is better to know the management of your own life than that of public grain.

4 Recall that this strength of the mind is highly capable of handling great matters, even though it is drawn to an honorable service that is not very suited for a happy life. Think back to how you have dedicated yourself since your youth to the study of the liberal arts, so that many thousands of bushels of grain would be entrusted to you; you had promised something greater and higher for yourself. There will also be people who are frugal and hardworking; slow pack animals are much better suited for carrying loads than noble horses, whose agile nature has never been burdened by a heavy load. 5 Consider, too, how much worry it is to face such a large burden; you have a human body to take care of. The hungry people are not swayed by reason, nor softened by fairness, nor influenced by any prayer. Just recently, during those few days when Gaius Caesar died, if anyone in the underworld had any sense, they would have grieved deeply knowing that the surviving Roman people had certainly only enough food for seven or eight days while he was joining bridges with ships and playing with the power of his command, the ultimate disaster was looming even for those besieged: the scarcity of food; it almost led to destruction and famine, followed by the ruin of everything, a furious mimicry of a foreign and unfortunately proud king. 6 What spirit did those have, who were entrusted with the care of public grain, when they were about to face Gaius? 1 Bring yourself back to these calmer, safer, greater matters!

You think it is similar whether you ensure that grain is transferred into the granaries, free from corruption by those who transport it and from negligence, so that it does not spoil due to moisture and heat, and that it responds in measurement and weight, or whether you approach these sacred and lofty matters to know what the essence of God is, what pleasure, what condition, what form; what awaits your mind in misfortune; where nature will compose us after we are freed from our bodies; what it is that supports the heaviest things in this world in the middle, suspends the light ones above, carries them into the highest fire, and stirs the stars in their cycles; and what else follows, filled with immense wonders? Do you wish to abandon the earth and focus your mind on these things? Now, while your blood is warm, it is time to strive for better things. A wealth of good arts awaits you in this life, the love of virtues and practice, the forgetfulness of desires, the knowledge of living and dying, and the deep peace of things. 

The condition of all those engaged is indeed miserable; however, the most miserable are those who do not even work in their own occupations, but sleep in another's dreams, walk in another's steps, and are commanded to love and hate, which are the freest of all things. If they wish to know how brief their own life is, let them consider what part of it belongs to them. 

When you see the robe often already taken up, and a famous name in the forum, do not envy; those things are prepared at the expense of life. As one of those years is counted, they waste all their years. Some, before they could rise to the height of ambition, were left behind in the prime of their struggling youth; others, after they had clawed their way to the summit of dignity through a thousand indignities, are sadly left to contemplate that they labored for a tombstone title; for some, old age ends while they are arranging new hopes as if they were young, and in the midst of great and wicked attempts, they weakly fail.

2 That pact, which in court sought the support of the elderly and inexperienced in the most obscure lawsuits, left him; that disgraceful man, who collapsed from exhaustion more quickly than from work itself; that disgraceful man, who, while being pulled into endless arguments, laughed at his heirs for a long time. 3 I cannot pass over the example that comes to mind. S. Turannius was an old man of precise diligence, who after his ninetieth year, when he had willingly accepted a break from his duties from C. Caesar, ordered himself to be laid in bed and, as if lifeless, commanded his family around him to mourn. The house mourned for the leisure of its elderly master and did not cease its sadness until his work was restored to him. Is it truly joyful to die while busy? 4 The same mindset prevails for many; their desire for work lasts longer than their ability to work; they struggle against the frailty of their bodies, and judge old age to be heavy solely by the fact that it separates them from activity. The law does not enroll a soldier after the age of fifty, nor does it summon a senator after sixty; it is harder for people to obtain leisure from themselves than from the law. 5 Meanwhile, while they are seized and seizing, while one breaks the other's peace, while they are mutually miserable, life exists without fruit, without pleasure, without any advancement of the soul. No one in the spotlight regards death, no one looks far ahead with hope; some even plan for what lies beyond life, great tombs and dedications of public works, gifts at the pyre, and grand funerals. But by Hercules, those funerals of theirs, as if they lived the least bit, must be led to flames and candles.
