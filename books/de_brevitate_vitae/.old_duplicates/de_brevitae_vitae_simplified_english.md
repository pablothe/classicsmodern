I. review  
[1] Most people, Paulinus, complain about the badness of nature, saying we are born into a very short life, that the time we have goes by too fast, and that except for a few, life does not give the rest of us what we want in how we live. It is not just the common people who complain about this bad situation; even important people have shared their worries. That is why famous doctors say: 'Life is short, but art lasts'; [2] and why Aristotle, a wise man studying nature, argues that nature gives animals a life of five or ten centuries, but for humans, who are part of so many great things, it gives a much shorter time. [3] We do not lack time; we just waste it.

That is why famous doctors say, "Life is short, but art lasts forever." This connects to Aristotle's strange argument, which seems odd for a wise person studying nature: "Animals can live for five or ten centuries, but for humans, who are born into so many great things, there is a much shorter limit." We do not lack time; we just waste it. A long life is given to us for doing great things if we use it well. But when we let it slip away through luxury and carelessness, when we do not do anything good, we will feel that time has passed without us even noticing it, pushed by the end we all face. In truth, we do not get a short life; we make it short, and we are not without time; we just waste it. Just like great wealth, when it is in the hands of a bad owner, is quickly lost, even small resources can grow well if taken care of by a good person. So, our time offers a lot to those who manage it wisely. What do we complain about when it comes to how things are? Life has been good to us; if you know how to use it, life is long.

It has treated us well; life, if you know how to enjoy it, is long. But some people are trapped by endless greed, while others are busy with pointless work; one drinks too much wine, another is lazy and does nothing; one is tired from wanting others’ approval, another is pushed by a careless wish to trade everywhere, hoping for profit; some suffer from wanting military honor, always worried about the dangers facing others or concerned about their own safety; there are those who are drained by serving ungrateful bosses, stuck in voluntary servitude; many are stopped by pretending to enjoy others' success or by complaining about their own lives; most are tossed around by uncertain feelings, unhappy with themselves and pulled into new plans; for some, nothing makes them happy enough to guide their path, and they feel overwhelmed by their fading and weak fates. I believe what the greatest poet said like a wise oracle: "We live a small part of life." Also, it is true that all space is not life, but time. Bad habits close in from all sides, keeping us from rising or seeing the truth, but they weigh us down, lost in desire.

Vices surround us on all sides, stopping us from rising or looking up to the truth. Instead, they keep us stuck in our desires. They can never find peace; whenever they have a chance to rest, like a deep sea after a storm, they are tossed around and never calm their longings. Do you think I’m talking about people whose wrongdoings are clear? Look at those who seem to have happiness; they are weighed down by their own wealth. So many are troubled by riches! Many people's need to show off their talents drains their energy! How many become weak from constant pleasures! How many end up with nothing because of the crowd around them! In the end, everyone, from the lowest to the highest, wanders about: here someone is trying to help, there someone is present, one is in danger, another defends, another judges, and no one claims their own identity; one person is consumed by another. If you ask about those whose names are remembered, you will see they are known by these signs: that one follows that one, this one follows another; no one belongs to themselves. Then there is the foolish anger of some: they complain about how their superiors look down on them, who do not have time for them even if they want to get closer! Does anyone dare to complain about someone else's pride, who never makes time for themselves?

Does anyone have the courage to complain about someone else’s pride, who never takes time for themselves? Yet, that person has looked at you, no matter who you are, with a proud face but has sometimes looked away; that person has listened to your words and welcomed you to their side; you have never chosen to look at yourself or listen. So, there is no reason to blame anyone for these duties because, when you did those things, you did not want to be with someone else but could not be with yourself.

III. To summarize  
Even though all the smart people who ever lived may agree on this one thing, they will always be amazed by the darkness of human minds. They do not let anyone take their things, and if there is a small fight about the borders, they quickly grab stones and weapons; they let others invade their lives, and in fact, they even invite those who will take ownership of it. No one can be found who wants to share their money; everyone tries to give their life to as many people as possible! They are focused on keeping their inheritance, while at the same time, they waste time, being the most wasteful in that, whose honest greed is unique.

They are focused on keeping their inheritance, but at the same time, they waste time by being very extravagant, driven by their own unique greed. It is good to choose someone from among the elders: “We see that you have lived a long life; you are now over a hundred years old. Let’s look at your age. Think about how much time you owe—how much to friends, how much to the king, how much to customers, how much to legal fights with your wife, how much to managing servants, and how much to your busy walking around the city. Add the sicknesses we have caused ourselves and also the time we have wasted; you will see that you have fewer years than you think. Remember when you were sure of your choices, how many days went by as you planned, when you were truly focused, when you felt carefree, when you were brave, what you have done in such a long life, how many have taken your time without you knowing what you were losing, how much empty pain, silly joy, greedy wishes, and nice talks have taken away, and how little is left for you; you will realize that you might die too soon.”

So, what is the reason?

Remember when you were sure about your choices, how many days went by as you wanted, when you were truly involved, when your face was happy, when you were brave, what you have done in your long life, how many have taken your life from you without you knowing what you were losing, how much empty pain, silly happiness, greedy wishes, and nice talks have taken away, and how little is left for you; you will see that you might die too soon.

So what is the reason? You live as if you will always be alive, never thinking about how fragile you are, not seeing how much time has already gone by; like a full cup, you are losing it, maybe while that day given to someone—whether a person or a thing—is the last. You want everything. You will hear many say: "I will stop working at fifty, the sixtieth year will free me from my duties." And what do you really get from living longer? Who will let you do what you plan? Are you not embarrassed to keep the last parts of life for yourself, spending only that time which cannot be used for anything good? How late it is to start living when it is time to end! What a silly forgetfulness of death to wait to make good plans until you are fifty-five or sixty and then want to begin life, which few have done!

What a silly mistake to forget about death and wait until you are fifty-five or sixty to start living, which few people have done!

IV. To tell  
[1] You will hear the voices of powerful men who want free time, praise it, and think it is better than all their riches. At the same time, they want to step down from their high positions if it is safe; because luck can change by itself, not affected by outside things. [2] The great Augustus, who got more from the gods than anyone else, never stopped asking for peace and a break from government work; his talks were always about hoping for free time. This idea made him feel good, even if it was just a dream, that one day he could live for himself. [3] In a letter to the Senate, he promised that his rest would still have dignity and would not be different from his past glory. He wrote: "But these things seem nicer to do than to promise. Yet my wish for this desired time makes me think that since the joy of my work still stays, I should enjoy the sweetness of words." [4] Free time seemed so important that, since he could not have it, he thought about it. He believed everything depended on him alone, who gave luck to people and nations, and he dreamed of the happiest day when he could let go of his greatness.

He saw everything depending on him alone, who gave good luck to people and countries, and he thought of the happiest day when he would let go of his power. He had learned how much those shining treasures in all lands required hard work and how much they hid hidden worries. First with the citizens, then with his friends, and finally pushed by family, he fought with weapons at sea and on land, spilling blood. Through Macedonia, Sicily, Egypt, Syria, Asia, and almost all coasts, he led his tired armies, worn from Roman fighting, to foreign battles. While the Alps were calm and enemies were being controlled during this peace and power, while he even moved the borders beyond the Rhine, Euphrates, and Danube, in the very city, the weapons of Murena, Caepio, Lepidus, Egnatius, and others were being sharpened against him. He had not yet escaped their traps: his daughter and many noble young men, driven by betrayal as if by a promise, were already frightening him in his old age, and once again, the woman he feared was with Antony. These wounds had cut him deeply: more were emerging; it was as if a heavy body was always breaking apart with much blood from some part. So, he wanted peace, and in this hope and thought, his efforts were resting; this was the promise of someone who could keep such a promise. M.

M. Cicero, caught between enemies like Catilina and Clodius, and uncertain friends like Pompey and Crassus, was struggling with the problems of the state. Things were going badly for him. He was recently taken away, neither happy with good times nor able to handle bad ones. He hated his own consulship, and he had good reason to complain endlessly! His words in a letter to Atticus after Pompey lost to the enemy were very sad. He wrote about his troubles while his son was still fighting in Spain. "What should I do," he asked, "here, you want to know? I stay in my half-free Tusculum." He then said more, regretful about his past, unhappy with now, and worried about the future. Cicero called himself half-free. But I swear by Hercules, a wise person will never accept such a low label. They will never be half-free; they will always have full and strong freedom, free and in control, better than others. For who can be above someone who is above luck?

### Review

Livius Drusus, a strong and passionate man, started new laws and stirred up the troubles of the Gracchi while surrounded by a large crowd from all over Italy. Not seeing how things would end, which he could not change or leave unfinished, he is said to have cursed his restless life from the start, saying he had never had a break, not even as a child.

Not seeing how events would turn out, which he could not change or leave unfinished, he is said to have cursed his restless life from the start, saying he never had a break, not even as a child. He even dared to speak on behalf of people in court while still young and to use his influence so well that some decisions were said to have come from him. How could such early ambition not explode? You would know that this reckless boldness would cause much harm, both to himself and to others. Later, he regretted never having a break since he was a child, being quarrelsome and heavy in the court. It is discussed whether he took his own life; he suddenly collapsed from a wound to his groin, and some wonder if his death was on purpose, with no one knowing if it was at the right time. There is no need to mention the many who seemed happier than him; they themselves spoke true about their lives, tired of everything they did, but these complaints changed nothing for anyone. His words came out quickly, and his feelings went back to what they were used to. By Hercules, your life, even if it lasts a thousand years, will be pulled into tight limits; these vices will take over every age.

By Hercules, your life, even if it lasts a thousand years, will be stuck in a small space; these bad habits will take over every age. This time, which nature goes through, reason grows, must quickly get away from you; because you neither understand nor hold on to it, nor do you slow down the fastest thing of all, but you let it go as if it were unimportant and fixable.
