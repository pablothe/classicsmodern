He heard the female shrieks. He had wanted to go back, bewildered.
Why had he turned onto the -sky Prospect, when suddenly, in one of the open windows of the tavern, he saw Svidryгайlov sitting at the window, at the tea table, with a pipe in his mouth. It was terrible, shockingly struck him. Svidryгайlov observed and examined him in silence, and what also immediately struck Raskolnikov, it seemed, he wanted to get up to quietly leave, before he was noticed. Raskolnikov immediately pretended that he hadn't noticed him and looked, pondering, in that direction, while continuing to observe him with his peripheral vision. His heart was beating anxiously. Indeed, Svidryгайlov, obviously, does not want to be seen. He moved the pipe from his lips and was about to hide; but, rising and pushing aside the chair, probably suddenly noticed that Raskolnikov was seeing and observing him. Something similar to the scene of their first meeting at Raskolnikov’s occurred between them. The scoundrel’s smile appeared on Svidryгайlov’s face and continued to expand. Both of them knew that both were seeing and observing each other. Finally, Svidryгайlov loudly laughed.

— Well, well! come in if you want; I’m here! — he shouted from the window.

Raskolnikov went up to the tavern.

He found him in a very small back room, with one window adjoining the large hall, where merchants, officials, and a multitude of people were drinking tea at twenty small tables, with the desperate chorus of singers crying out, the sound of billiard balls striking. A bottle of champagne stood on the table in front of Svidryгайlov, and a glass half full of wine. The room also contained a little organist with a small hand organ.

A healthy, fair-skinned girl in a striped, puckered skirt and a Tyrolean hat with ribbons, a singer, about eighteen years old, who, despite the choral song in another room, sang to the accompaniment of an organist, with a rather slurred countertenor, some household song…

— And quite enough! — interrupted her Svidryгайlov at the entrance of Raskolnikov.

The girl immediately stopped and stood in a respectful expectation. She was singing her rhymed servant’s song with some serious and respectful expression on her face.

— Hey, Philip, a glass! — shouted Svidryгайlov.

— I will not drink wine, — said Raskolnikov.

— As you wish, I am not for you. Drink, Katya! Nothing more will be needed, go forth! — He poured her a whole glass of wine and laid out a yellowish ticket. Katya drank the glass in one go, as women drink wine, that is, without pausing, in twenty gulps, took the ticket, kissed Svidryгайlov’s hand, which he very seriously allowed to be kissed, and left the room, and behind her, a boy with an organ dragged himself. Both were brought from the street. Svidryгайlov had not lived in Petersburg for weeks, and everything around him was on some patriarchal leg. The tavern servant, Philip, was also already “familiar” and became obsequious. The door to the hall was locked; Svidryгайlov was in this room as if at home and spent in it, perhaps, whole days. The tavern was dirty, bad, and even not of average hand.
