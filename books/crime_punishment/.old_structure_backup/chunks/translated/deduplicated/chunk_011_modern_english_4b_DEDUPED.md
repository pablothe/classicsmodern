That evening of the same day, when the barracks had already been locked, Raskolnikov lay on the floor and thought about her. On that day he even felt that all the exiles, his former enemies, were looking at him differently. He even spoke to them himself, and they answered him kindly. He now remembered this, but it should have been so: shouldn't everything now change?

He thought about her. He remembered how he had constantly tormented and ravaged her heart; he remembered her poor, thin face, but these memories no longer tormented him so much: he knew with what infinite love he would now redeem all her sufferings.

And what are all these, all the torments of the past! Everything, even his crime, even the sentence and exile, seemed to him now, in the first impulse, like some external, strange, as if even not having happened to him, fact. He, however, could not that evening long and constantly think about something, concentrate on something with his mind; he would not even now consciously resolve anything; he only felt. Instead of dialectics, life had come, and something completely different had to develop in his consciousness.

Under his pillow lay the Gospel. He picked it up mechanically. This book belonged to her, it was the same one from which she read to him about the resurrection of Lazarus. At the beginning of the exile he thought that she would torture him with religion, would talk about the Gospel and impose books on him. He gave her the Gospels. He asked for them from her shortly before his illness, and she silently brought him a book. He still hasn't opened it.

He hasn't opened it now, but one thought flashed through him: “Can her beliefs not now be my beliefs? Her feelings, her aspirations, at least…”

She was also in a state of excitement all day, and even fell ill that night. But she was so happy that she almost feared her own happiness. Seven years, only seven years! In the beginning of her happiness, in certain moments, they were both ready to look at these seven years as if they were seven days. He didn't even know that new life wasn't given to him for free, that it had to be bought at a great, future feat…

But now a new story begins, a story of the gradual renewal of a person, a story of the gradual rebirth of him, a gradual transition from one world to another, acquaintance with a new, previously unknown reality. This could form the subject of a new story – but our present story is finished.
