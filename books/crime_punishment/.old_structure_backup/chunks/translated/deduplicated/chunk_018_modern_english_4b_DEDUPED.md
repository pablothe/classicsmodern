On the contrary, he was even glad to work: having exhausted himself physically at work, he at least obtained several hours of quiet sleep. And what did food mean to him – these empty porridge with cockroaches? As a student, during his previous life, he often did not have it. His dress was warm and adapted to his lifestyle. He even did not feel shackles on himself. Should he be ashamed of his shorn head and half-coat? But before whom? Before Sonya? Sonya feared him, and was it before her that he should be ashamed?

And what was it? He was ashamed even before Sonya, who tormented him with this scornful and rude treatment. But not the shorn head and shackles he…

He was ashamed: his pride was severely wounded; he fell ill from the wounded pride. Oh, how happy he would have been if he could have blamed himself alone! He would have endured then everything, even shame and disgrace. But he strictly judged himself, and the fierce conscience did not find any particularly terrible guilt in his past, except for merely a simple mistake which could happen with anyone. He was ashamed precisely of the fact that he, Raskolnikov, perished so blindly, hopelessly, deafly and stupidly, by some decree of a blind fate, and must accept and submit to the “nonsense” of some decree, if he wanted to calm himself down.

The pointless and aimless anxiety in the present, and in the future – a continuous sacrifice, which nothing was gained – this was what awaited him in the world. And that in the fact that after eight years he will only be thirty-two years old and can start living again! Why should he live? What should he have in mind? To strive for what? To live in order to exist? But he a thousand times and before was ready to give his existence for an idea, for a hope, even for a fantasy. One existence was always not enough for him; he always wanted something more. Perhaps, by virtue of the force of his desires, he considered himself then a man to whom it is more permitted than to another.

And even if fate sent him repentance – a burning repentance, shattering the heart, driving away sleep, such repentance from whose terrible torments one sees a loop and a pit! Oh, he would rejoice in it! Torments and tears – this is also life. But he did not repent his crime.

At least he could be angry at his own stupidity, as he had been before at his improper and silliest actions which had brought him to Ostrog. But now, already in Ostrog, free, he discussed and considered all his past actions again and did not find them so stupid and improper as they had seemed to him in that fateful time, before.

“What, what,” he thought, “was my thought dumber than other thoughts and theories, breeding and colliding one with another in the world, since that light appeared? It is only necessary to look at the matter with a completely independent, broad and free from everyday influences view, and then, of course, my thought will be far from so… strange. To the deniers and wise men, for a copper coin, why do you stop halfway!

What does their action seem so improper to them?” he said to himself. “That it is a crime? What does the word ‘crime’ mean? My conscience is calm. Of course, a criminal offense has been committed; of course, the letter of the law has been violated and blood has been spilled, well, take the letter of the law for my head… and that’s it! Of course, in that case even many benefactors of humanity, who did not inherit power but seized it themselves, should have been executed at their very first steps. But those people took their steps, and therefore they are right, and I did not take mine and, therefore, I had no right to allow myself this step.”

This is where he admitted his crime: only in that he did not bear it and confessed with a guilty plea.

He also suffered from the thought: why didn't he kill himself then? Why did he prefer to appear with a confession instead of standing over the river? Was there such strength in this desire to live that it so easily overcame him? It overcame Svidrigailov, who feared death?

He tormented himself with this question and could not understand that even then, when he stood over the river, he might have sensed deep lies within himself and in his convictions. He did not understand that this premonition could have been a harbinger of the future upheaval in his life, his future resurrection, his future new outlook on life.

He more readily admitted only that stupid burden of instinct, which he could not tear himself away from and through which he was again unable to step over (due to weakness and insignificance). He looked at his fellow prisoners and was amazed: how did they all love life, how did they cherish it! It seemed to him that they loved and valued it even more acutely in the midst of suffering than they did in freedom. What terrible tortures and punishments had others endured, for example, the vagrants! Was one single ray of sunshine, a dense forest, somewhere in the unknown wilderness, a cold spring, marked since the third year, and about which the vagrant dreamed as he dreamed of a lover, seen in his dreams, green grass around it, a singing bird in the bush? Looking further, he saw even more inexplicable examples.

In the penal colony, in its surrounding environment, he, of course, noticed many things, and he did not want to notice them at all. He lived, as it were, with his eyes lowered: it was disgusting for him to…

It was unbearable to look at. But by the end, many things began to surprise him, and he, unwillingly, began to notice things he had previously not suspected. In general, the terrible, impassable abyss that lay between him and all this people surprised him the most. It seemed that he and they were of different nations. He and they looked at each other distrustfully and with hostility. He knew and understood the common causes of such separation; but he had never before allowed himself to believe that these causes were actually so deep and strong. There were also exiles-Poles in the fortress, political criminals. They simply considered all this people to be ignoramuses and serfs and despised them from above; but Raskolnikov could not look at them in that way: he clearly saw that these ignoramuses were much wiser than these Poles. There were also Russians here, who also despised this people too – one former officer and two seminary students; Raskolnikov clearly noticed their mistake.

He himself was disliked and avoided by everyone. They even began to hate him at the end – why? He did not know this. They scorned him, laughed at him, laughed at his crime those who were much more guilty than him.

He lay in the hospital throughout the end of the fast and Holy Week. Already recovering, he remembered his dreams when he was lying in the heat and in delirium. He dreamed that the whole world was sacrificed to some terrible, unheard-of, and unseen ulcer, coming from the depths of Asia to Europe. Everyone was to die, except for some, very few, chosen ones. Some new trichinids appeared, microscopic creatures, inhabiting the bodies of people. But these creatures were spirits, gifted with intelligence and will. People who accepted them into themselves immediately became mad and insane. But never, never did people consider themselves so smart and unshakable in truth as the infected considered themselves. They never considered themselves more unshakable in their judgments, their scientific conclusions, their moral beliefs and faiths. Entire villages, entire cities and peoples were infected and going mad. Everyone was in alarm and did not understand each other, everyone thought that the truth was in one of them, and suffered, looking at others, beating their chests, crying and breaking their hands. They didn’t know whom or what to judge, they couldn’t agree on what was evil, what was good. They didn’t know whom to accuse, whom to excuse. People killed each other in some senseless malice. They gathered on each other in entire armies, but armies, already in motion, suddenly began to torment themselves, ranks were disrupted.

The warriors rushed at each other, wounded themselves, slashed themselves, bit each other, and ate each other.

In the cities, a bell was constantly beaten all day long: everyone was summoned, but no one knew who or for what reason was calling, and everyone was in alarm. Ordinary crafts were abandoned, because everyone offered their thoughts and corrections, and they could not agree; agriculture stopped. People gathered in heaps in some places, agreed on something together, swore not to part – but immediately began something completely different than they had just imagined, began to accuse each other, fought and slashed themselves. Fires began, and famine began. Everyone and everything perished. The ulcer grew and moved further and further.

Only a few people could save themselves in the whole world, these were the pure and chosen, destined to begin a new race of people and a new life, to renew and cleanse the earth, but no one and nowhere had seen these people, no one had heard their words and voices.
