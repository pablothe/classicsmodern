— Katerina Ivanovna is suffering from consumption, she is angry; she will soon die, — said Raskolnikov, after a pause and without answering the question.

— Oh, no, no, no! — And Sonya, with an unconscious gesture, grabbed him by both hands, as if pleading with him, “no.”

— But it’s better if she dies.

— No, not better, not better, not at all better! — she repeated fearfully and without reason.

— And what about the children? Where will you take them then, if not to you?

— Oh, I don’t know! — Sonya exclaimed almost in despair and grabbed at her head. It was clear that this thought had flashed through her many, many times, and he had only startled it again.

— Well, if you, while Katerina Ivanovna is still alive, now you get sick and they take you to the hospital, what then will happen? — he insisted mercilessly.

— Oh, don’t, don’t, don’t! That cannot be! — and Sonya’s face twisted with a terrible fright.

— How can that be? — Raskolnikov continued with a stiff smirk, — are you not insured? Then what will happen to them? They will all go out in a throng, she will cough and ask, and bang her head against a wall somewhere, like today, and the children will cry… And then she will fall, they will take her to the barracks, to the hospital, she will die, and the children…

— Oh, no!.. God will not allow this! — it finally burst out from her tightly clenched chest, Sonya. She listened, looking at him with a plea and folding her hands in a silent request, as if everything depended on him.

Raskolnikov rose and began to walk around the room. A minute passed. Sonya stood with her hands and head lowered, in terrible anguish.

— Can’t one accumulate? Can one save for a black day? — he asked, suddenly stopping before her.

— No, — whispered Sonya.

— Of course, no! — he added, almost with a sneer. — Have you tried?

— I have tried.

— And it failed! Well, of course! What’s to ask about it!

And he walked around the room again. Another minute passed.

— Don’t you get it every day?

Sonya was even more confused than before, and color flushed her face again.

— No, — she whispered with a painful effort.

— It will be the same with Polechka, — he said suddenly.

— No! no! It cannot be, no! — Sonya screamed, as if suddenly wounded with a knife. — God, God will not allow such horror!

— Others are allowed.

— No, no! Her God will protect her, God! — she repeated, distraught.

— Yes, maybe there is no God at all, — Raskolnikov replied with some even malicious glee, laughed, and looked at her.

Sonya’s face suddenly changed frightfully: convulsions ran across it. She looked at him with an unutterable reproach, wanted to say something, but couldn’t utter a word and suddenly, bitterly, sobbed, covering her face with her hands.

— You say that Katya Ivanovna’s mind is going astray; your own mind

He said after a moment of silence that his mind is mixed up.

Five minutes passed. He walked back and forth in silence, not looking at her. Finally he approached her; his eyes were gleaming. He took her by both shoulders and looked directly at her weeping face. His gaze was dry, inflamed, sharp, his lips were trembling violently… Suddenly he quickly leaned over and, kneeling to the floor, kissed her foot. Sonya recoiled from him as if from a madman. And indeed, he looked as if he were completely mad.

“What are you doing, what are you doing? In front of me!” she muttered, paling, and her heart suddenly clenched painfully.

He immediately stood up.

“I did not bow to you, I bowed to all human suffering,” he said wildly and went to the window. “Listen,” he added, turning to her a minute later, “I recently told one bully that he wasn’t worth one of your little fingers… and that I paid my sister homage today by sitting her next to you.”

“Oh, what did you say that to her! And right there with her!” Sonya exclaimed fearfully, “sitting with me! Homage! But I… dishonorable… I am great, great sinner!” “Oh, what did you say that to her!”
